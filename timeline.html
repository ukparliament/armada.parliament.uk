Tapestries

1556

Queen Mary Tudor married Philip II of Spain

When married to Queen Mary Tudor (1516–1558), Philip II of Spain spent money on ship building in England, improving the English fleet.

1558

Queen Elizabeth I’s accession to the throne of England

1568

Spanish invaded the Netherlands

The Spanish invaded the Netherlands to quell Protestant rebels.

1585

Elizabeth sent Sir Francis Drake to attack Spanish ports

The Duke of Parma had almost beaten the Dutch, so Elizabeth supported them with money and soldiers. She also sent Sir Francis Drake to attack Spanish ports in the West Indies.

1585

Philip II of Spain planned to attack

Philip II of Spain formed the intention to invade. His project was called ‘the Enterprise of England’.

1586

Philip II prepared invasion

Philip II prepared his land invasion force and the ships of the Armada.

9th April 1587

Sir Francis Drake attacked Cadiz

Drake’s attack on Cadiz; 35 Spanish ships lost or damaged.

1587

Elizabeth imprisoned & Mary Queen of Scots beheaded

Elizabeth's imprisonment and execution of Mary Queen of Scots infuriated Philip II and reaffirmed his purpose to attack.

20th January 1588

Commander of the Armada died

Philip II’s Commander-in-Chief, Admiral Don Alvaro de Bazan, Marquis of Santa Cruz (1626-1588) died suddenly. Don Alonso Perez de Guzman, Duke of Medina Sidonia (1550-1619) was to take his place as leader of the Armada.

20th May 1588

The Spanish Armada set sail from Lisbon.

Summer 1588

English prepare for Spanish attack

Believing that the Spanish would make an attack on London, Elizabeth I and her council had the Earl of Essex make camp under the Earl of Leicester’s leadership, to protect the city. English navy captains awaited the Spanish attack at Plymouth and Dover, backed-up by trained bands of militia along the south coast.

9th June 1588

Bad weather stalled the Armada

Hit by bad weather, the Armada put into Corunna.

12th July 1588

Armada resumed its journey

Setting sail from Corunna, the Armada resumed its journey to attack England, passing through poor weather on the way.

19th July 1588

The Armada was sighted of the coast of England.

First Engagement between the Spanish and English Fleets

23rd July 1588

‘The Engagement of Both Fleets against the Isle of Portland’

by Anthony Oakshett, oil and gold leaf on canvas, 2010

23rd July 1588

‘Some English Ships attack the Spanish Fleet to the Westward. The Spaniards draw themselves into a Roundel: And afterwards keeping on their Course, are followed by the English’

by John Pine, engraving, 1739

25th July 1588

‘The Sharpest Engagement against the Isle of Wight’

by Anthony Oakshett, oil and gold leaf on canvas, 2010

26th - 27th July 1588

'The Spanish Fleet is seen sailing up the Channel, intending to stop at Dunkirk or Calais, where they were to be join'd by the Duke of Parma: The English follow them close'

Engraving by John Pine, 1739

28th - 29th July 1588

‘English Fireships Dislodge the Spanish Fleet before Calais’

30th July 1588
31st July 1588

Strong winds aided English defence

Strong winds and the English almost succeeded in running the Armada aground off Zeeland.

1st – 2nd August 1588

English pursued Spanish attackers

The English pursued the Spanish as they sailed up the North Sea to the Firth of Forth.

3rd August 1588

English ships turned back

8th August 1588

Elizabeth addressed the army

Elizabeth is recorded as giving a rousing speech to the army, saying:

‘I know I have the Body but of a weak and feeble Woman, but I have the Heart and Stomach of a King, and of a King of England too.’ (quoted by John Pine, 1739)

Late September 1588

Armada sailed home

After a difficult journey during which many men lost their lives, the surviving Armada ships returned home.

Late 1580s

Sea charts commissioned, late 1580s / early 1590s

Lord Howard of Effingham commissioned a set of 11 charts of the Armada attack from the designer and cartographer Robert Adams (died 1595).

1580s

Battle depicted in sea charts

Cartographer Robert Adams (died 1595) drew sea charts depicting the battle for Lord Howard of Effingham.

1590

Sea charts published

Augustine Ryther (died 1593) engraved and published Adams’s charts in ‘Expeditionis Hispanorum in Angliam ver descripto, Anno Do MDLXXXVIII’

1592

Tapestries commissioned

Lord Howard of Effingham commissioned the ten Armada Tapestries from a Dutchman, Francis Spierincx (1549/51–1630). Spierincx employed his countryman Hendrick Cornelisz Vroom (1566–1640), a naval artist, to design the tapestries.

1595

Tapestries completed

Completed at a cost of £1,582, the tapestries were hung in Effingham's London residence.

1602

Queen Elizabeth viewed the tapestries

1616

King James I purchased the tapestries for £1,628.

Before 1649

‘Queen Elizabeth I, the Twisden Portrait’

King Charles I is reputed to have given the ‘Armada’ portrait of Elizabeth I to Judge Twisden.

1640s

Trial of Archbishop William Laud (1573-1645)

1644

Tapestries hung in the House of Lords

The first appearance of the tapestries as hangings in the House of Lords. According to the House of Lords Journal at the time

‘the Lieutenant of The Tower of London is hereby Ordered to deliver to the Officers of the King’s Standing Wardrobe, the Suite of Hangings of the Story of eighty-Eight to hang up in the Lords House of Parliament.’

1651

The tapestries were probably on permanent display in the House of Lords.

1707

Act of Union with Scotland

1730s

John Pine undertook the task of making an engraving of each of the Armada tapestries and the sea charts.

1735

The Engraving and Copyright Act was passed.

1739

Pine's set of engravings were published.

1739

Lord Chesterfield objected to a Spanish treaty

In a debate in 1739 Lord Chesterfield objected to a treaty with Spain, declaring,

‘these walls, my Lords, ought to put us in mind of the methods by which our Ancestors preserved the Trade, and vindicated the Honour of the Nation’.

7th April 1778

Earl of Chatham died

The Earl of Chatham collapsed and died in the Chamber, in a speech in which he was arguing for the War of Independence against America.

1798

Tapetries used to satirize French attack threat

James Gillray’s (1757-1815) caricature ‘Consequences of a successful French invasion’ showed how important the Armada tapestries had become as national symbols. The threat of a French attack on Britain was satirized in a scene showing the tapestries being destroyed.

Early 1800s

‘The Court of Requests at State Opening or Prorogation’

by an unknown artist, watercolour, early 19th century

1801

'House of Lords (showing the Court of Requests)'

Following the enlargement of the peerage after the union with Ireland, the House of Lords moved from the Parliament Chamber to the more spacious Court of Requests. Eight tapestries were hung, one of which was behind the throne.

1820

‘The Trial of Queen Caroline, 1820’

For the trial of Queen Caroline of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel (1768-1821) special galleries were installed, designed to protect the tapestries.

1831

‘The King Proroguing Parliament, April, 1831’

The tapestries appear as a backdrop to King William IV’s prorogation of Parliament at the end of a session.

1832

'The Reform Bill, 1832’

The Reform Act widened the franchise, making it possible for a wider section of the adult male population to cast their vote and elect Members of Parliament.

1830s

‘Interior of the House of Lords’

by John Shury, lithograph, undated

Paintings

16th October 1834

‘Fire 1834: View from Lambeth Shore of the Palace of Westminster’

The House of Lords was accidentally burnt down because of an overloaded furnace, and the tapestries were destroyed.

1836

Charles Barry won the competition to design the new Palace of Westminster, against 96 other entries.

1841

‘Queen Victoria with Justice and Clemency’

Royal Commission on Fine Arts was formed, chaired by Prince Albert. The schemes of art it commissioned for the Palace of Westminster illustrated British history, showing Queen Victoria as a model monarch.

1847

Prince’s Chamber theme chosen

Tudor history was chosen for the Prince’s Chamber. The scheme was to include a series of six paintings illustrating the defeat of the Spanish Armada.

1847

The new House of Lords Chamber opened.

Late 1850s

One painting completed

One painting from the series, 'The English fleet pursuing the Spanish fleet against Fowey', was completed by Richard Burchett, but never hung in the Prince’s Chamber.

1861

Prince Albert died

Work on the Palace's schemes of art slowed and within a few years the Fine Arts Commission had disbanded. The Armada paintings project was halted indefinitely.

Late 1907

A Select Committee Report noted the importance of completing the Armada paintings for the Prince's Chamber.

1914 - 1918

The First World War led to the plans for the Armada Paintings being suspended.

1988

Exhibition marked 400 years since the Armada

An international exhibition was held at the National Maritime Museum, Greenwich, London, ‘Armada 1588–1988’. Academics revealed that a quarter, rather than a half of the Armada ships had been lost.

2007

Proposal to complete the Armada paintings discussed

Following a generous donation to the House of Lords by Mark Pigott, KBE, a proposal to complete the series of Armada paintings was discussed.

The House of Lords Works of Art Committee agreed that the funds should be used to commission the remaining five Armada paintings for the Prince's Chamber and selected Anthony Oakshett as the lead artist.

Her Majesty The Queen gave her permission to hang the Armada paintings in the Prince's Chamber at the Palace of Westminster.

2007

The search for studio space began.

March 2008

Work of the Armada Project Board began

A decision was made on which five of the ten tapestries would be reproduced as paintings.

May 2008

A seminar was held at the House of Lords to help inform the project

Invited specialists on the Spanish Armada of 1588 attended from the UK and Europe.

June 2008

The painting project began at Wrest Park

Five blank canvases with gilded borders were delivered.

July 2008

Work to transfer the line drawings onto the canvases had begun.

October 2008

Canvases were filled with the base colours.

February 2009

Decorative detail was added to the gilded borders.

November 2009

Gilded lettering was added

Lettering to match Burchett’s alphabet was added, giving the names of those depicted in the portraits, and the titles of the paintings. Each letter was now painstakingly gilded, and a shadow painted-in to make the lettering stand out.

Winter 2009 & Spring 2010

Details such as the crests of waves, rigging and anchors were added

The final task was populating the ships' decks with hundreds of sailors.

2010

The Armada paintings were displayed in the Royal Gallery

HRH The Duke of York opened the exhibition. A major logistical project followed to transfer the canvases to their final location on the upper walls of the Princes Chamber. This completed the plan of 1847.
